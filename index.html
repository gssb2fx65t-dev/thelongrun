Sleep
Sleep is the highest-leverage thing
Your body does not get fitter during runs - it adapts during recovery, and that process is almost entirely sleep-dependent. During heavy training weeks (20+ miles) you likely need 30-60 min more than usual. Persistent fatigue that does not resolve after two easy days often means sleep debt, not overtraining.
Two nights before matters most
The night before Saturday long run matters less than Wednesday and Thursday nights. One bad Friday does not derail a long run nearly as much as poor mid-week sleep. Friday nights are often socially busier - worth knowing.
Alcohol and sleep quality
Alcohol disrupts sleep depth even when it does not reduce hours. You get the hours but not the restorative depth. Particularly relevant the night before any hard effort.
Nutrition
Protein timing
The 30-60 min window after Saturday long run is when muscles are most receptive to protein. Eating one of your meal prep containers Saturday afternoon is genuinely useful - aim for 25-40g. During heavier training weeks, target 100-130g protein per day total.
Do not run fasted on long runs
As Saturday distances grow past 7-8 miles, running without breakfast compromises quality and recovery. A simple breakfast 60-90 minutes before is enough. Do not skip this.
Carbs are your fuel
During weeks above 15 miles, carbohydrates are your primary fuel. Restricting them makes runs harder, slows recovery, and raises injury risk. The grain pouches in meal prep are doing important work - do not second-guess them.
Anti-inflammatory eating
During Phase 2 build and HM build, salmon weekly is genuinely valuable - omega-3s are anti-inflammatory. Ultra-processed foods and excess alcohol amplify the inflammatory response to training load.
Eat enough
Appetite suppresses after long runs so under-fueling is common. Signs: persistent fatigue, runs feeling inexplicably hard, getting sick during build phases. On days with a long run you need meaningfully more food than rest days.
Hydration - especially in winter
Indoor heating is extremely dehydrating. Light yellow urine in the morning = good. Dark yellow = you are behind. One of the most commonly overlooked things among recreational runners.

Training 2027

Oct 19 - Nov 15, 2026
Recovery
No targets
Run when you feel like it. Saturday and Tuesday remain natural anchors if you want structure, but neither distance nor frequency is required. 15-20 miles per week is a soft ceiling, not a goal. The only measure of success: enjoying running without obligation.
Nov 16 - Dec 31, 2026
Maintenance
~20 mi/wk
Three easy runs per week, roughly 20 miles. Saturday carries the long run (5-6 miles), Tuesday anchors the midweek (3 miles), and one optional day fills out the week. No targets beyond showing up.
January 2027
Mental Reset
No running
No running. You have been at this since March 2026 - ten months of consistent training. A full month off is not a failure; it is the reward. Come back in February wanting to run.
Feb 1 - Feb 28, 2027

~15 to 20 mi/wk
Rebuild the Saturday/Tuesday anchor structure at easy mileage across four weeks. Effort genuinely easy throughout.
